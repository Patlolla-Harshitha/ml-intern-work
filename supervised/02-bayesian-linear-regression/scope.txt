2
Subtopics
Core concepts and intuition

Why treat weights as random variables — quantifying belief before and after seeing data, and what it means to reason under uncertainty rather than commit to a single model.

Mathematical formulation (final equations only)

Closed-form expressions for the posterior mean μₙ, posterior covariance Σₙ, and predictive distribution p(y*|x*, X, y) — no derivations, just the equations and what each term means.

Prior, likelihood, and posterior distributions

Conjugate Gaussian prior over weights, Gaussian likelihood of observations, resulting Gaussian posterior — why conjugacy makes the math tractable and updates closed-form.

Implementation details

NumPy closed-form implementation, sequential Bayesian updates, using PyMC for probabilistic programming, and scikit-learn's BayesianRidge for production-ready inference.

Failure modes, edge cases, and misconceptions

Prior misspecification, ill-conditioned covariance matrices, conflating MAP with full Bayesian inference, and the misconception that BLR is "just Ridge with uncertainty."

3
Top 5 interview questions
Q1
Conceptual
How does Bayesian Linear Regression differ from OLS and Ridge Regression? Under what assumptions does each reduce to the other?

Tests: understanding of MAP ↔ Ridge equivalence under Gaussian prior, and full posterior vs. point estimate tradeoff.

Q2
Derivation
Walk me through how you arrive at the posterior distribution over weights. Why is the result Gaussian, and what are the closed-form expressions for its mean and covariance?

Tests: conjugacy, completing the square intuitively, understanding of Σₙ = (α I + β XᵀX)⁻¹ and μₙ = β Σₙ Xᵀy.

Q3
Conceptual
What does the predictive uncertainty represent in BLR, and why does it increase far from training data? How does this differ from aleatoric vs. epistemic uncertainty?

Tests: ability to decompose predictive variance into noise (aleatoric) and model uncertainty (epistemic), and interpret uncertainty estimates in deployment.

Q4
Implementation
How would you perform online/sequential Bayesian updates for a streaming data problem? What property of the posterior makes this computationally feasible?

Tests: understanding that the posterior at step n becomes the prior at step n+1 — elegant and practically important for real-time systems.

Q5
Design
When would you choose BLR over a Gaussian Process or a neural network with MC Dropout for a regression problem with limited data?

Tests: practical tradeoff reasoning — scalability, prior expressiveness, interpretability, computational cost, and uncertainty calibration in low-data regimes.

5
Connections to other ML concepts
← Generalises from

Ridge Regression

MAP estimate under Gaussian prior is exactly Ridge; BLR adds full uncertainty quantification.

→ Leads to

Gaussian Processes

BLR in infinite-dimensional feature space = GP. Same posterior predictive structure, kernel replaces inner product.

→ Motivates

Variational Inference

When posteriors aren't conjugate, VI approximates them — BLR is the tractable closed-form special case.

↔ Parallel idea

Kalman Filter

Sequential BLR update equations are structurally identical to the Kalman Filter predict/update cycle.

→ Extends to

Bayesian Neural Nets

Same philosophy — place priors over weights — but non-conjugate, requiring MCMC, VI, or MC Dropout.

↔ Related idea

Empirical Bayes

Marginal likelihood maximisation (Evidence framework) to tune α, β hyperparameters — avoids grid search.

→ Foundational for

Bayesian Optimisation

Surrogate models in BO (often GPs or BLR) use the same posterior predictive uncertainty to drive exploration.

← Conceptual basis

Regularisation

L2 regularisation has a direct Bayesian interpretation as a Gaussian prior — BLR makes this explicit.

→ Scales toward

MCMC / HMC

When closed-form posterior is unavailable (non-linear models), MCMC approximates it — BLR is the analytic baseline.
